Gambas al Ajillo

Trader Joe's Red Argentinian Shrimp — Spanish Garlic Style over Valentine's Salad — 2026-02-15

Phase 1: Mise en Place

10 min

Shrimp Prep Do First

Mise en Place

ItemAmountPrep
Red Argentinian shrimp454g (1 lb)Thawed, shell-off, deveined
Diamond Crystal kosher salt4gFor seasoning shrimp (~1 tsp DC)
Black pepper1gFresh cracked
Smoked paprika2g~1 tsp

Instructions

  1. Pat shrimp aggressively dry — lay on paper towels, press with more towels on top. Flip and repeat. Surface moisture is the enemy of sear.
  2. Season shrimp — toss with 4g salt, black pepper, and smoked paprika. Set aside on a plate in a single layer.
Maillard reaction requires surface temps above 300°F. Moisture = steam = gray, rubbery shrimp. The 60 seconds you spend drying is the most important step in this recipe (McGee, Lopez-Alt).
Red Argentinian shrimp are naturally red when raw — you cannot use color to judge doneness. Use shape: loose C = done, tight O = overcooked.

Aromatics & Fats

Mise en Place

ItemAmountPrep
Garlic5 cloves (~20g)Thinly sliced lengthwise, not minced
Extra virgin olive oil40g (~3 tbsp)—
Unsalted butter14g (~1 tbsp)—
Red pepper flakes1gPinch to ¼ tsp — adjust to heat preference

Instructions

  1. Slice garlic thin — lengthwise, ~2mm thick. Slices (not minced) so they toast into visible golden chips and don't burn instantly.
  2. Measure oil and butter — have them next to the stove. Butter goes in with the oil to raise the smoke point while adding flavor.
Sliced garlic gives you a wider window before burning vs. minced. Whole slices go from raw → golden → bitter over about 90 seconds. Minced does that in 30. You want golden, nutty, not acrid (Nosrat).
Everything prepped and next to the stove? Good. From here it's 5 minutes of active cooking. Don't walk away.

Phase 2: Build the Garlic Oil

2–3 min

Garlic & Chili in Cool Oil Low & Slow

Instructions

  1. Cold pan start — add 40g olive oil + 14g butter and the sliced garlic to a cold pan. Starting cold gives the garlic time to infuse the oil as the temp climbs.
  2. Heat to medium-low — let garlic sizzle gently. You want tiny, lazy bubbles — not rapid frying. Pan surface around 275–300°F.
  3. Toast until light gold — about 2 minutes. Edges just starting to color, kitchen smells incredible.
  4. Remove garlic chips — use a slotted spoon, set aside on a small plate. They'll go back in at the end.
  5. Add red pepper flakes — 1g into the hot garlic oil. Let bloom for 10 seconds. The oil will tint slightly and the flakes will darken.
Blooming spices in fat activates volatile compounds (terpenes, capsaicinoids) that are oil-soluble, not water-soluble. 10 seconds is enough — longer and the flakes go bitter (McGee, Sercarz). This infused oil IS the sauce.
Pull the garlic before it goes past light gold. Residual heat in the chips will carry them to perfect golden. Dark brown garlic = bitter garlic = start over.

Phase 3: Sear the Shrimp

3 min — fast & hot

The Sear High Heat

Instructions

  1. Crank heat to medium-high — let the garlic oil come up to temp. You want a shimmer, not smoking. Pan surface around 375–400°F.
  2. Lay shrimp in a single layer — do NOT crowd. If your pan is small, work in two batches. Each shrimp needs direct contact with the hot pan surface. No stacking, no overlapping.
  3. Sear side one — 90 seconds, untouched. Don't move them. You're building a Maillard crust on that first side. Look for the edges turning opaque about halfway up.
  4. Flip once — 60 seconds on side two. They cook faster now because the pan is hotter and the shrimp are already partially cooked through.
  5. Check doneness — target internal temp 120°F. Carryover will bring them to 130°F which is perfect: juicy, tender, slightly translucent center. Shape check: loose C = done. Tight O = overcooked.
  6. Kill the heat immediately.
These shrimp cook FAST — they're delicate deep-water shrimp, much softer than Gulf whites or tigers. At 140°F they're rubber bands. Pull early and trust the carryover. Use a thermometer if you have one (Lopez-Alt).
Temperature > time for proteins. "90 seconds per side" is a guideline, not gospel. If your shrimp are smaller or your pan is hotter, they'll be done sooner. Watch the shape, probe the temp (Food Lab).

Phase 4: Finish & Plate

1 min

Reunite & Sauce

Instructions

  1. Return garlic chips — toss them back into the pan with the shrimp. The residual heat will warm them without further cooking.
  2. Optional: squeeze of lemon — if you have one, half a lemon squeezed into the pan right now adds brightness that makes the garlic pop. Acid is the most underused finishing tool.
  3. Tilt the pan — spoon the garlic-chili butter-oil over the shrimp 3–4 times. This is your sauce. It's olive oil + butter + garlic + paprika + chili — liquid gold.
Nosrat's framework: if something tastes good but feels like it's missing something, it almost always needs acid. A squeeze of lemon here lifts the dish from "good garlic shrimp" to "restaurant garlic shrimp."

Over the Salad Serve Warm

Instructions

  1. Plate the Valentine's salad — divide onto plates or pile onto one platter.
  2. Arrange shrimp on top — warm, straight from the pan. Scatter the garlic chips visibly.
  3. Spoon the pan sauce over everything — the warm garlic oil will slightly wilt the greens where it hits. This is a feature, not a bug — it's a warm dressing now.
Done. 1 pound of shrimp is perfect for two over salad. The warm pan sauce replaces any dressing you'd normally use — don't add vinaigrette on top of this.

Quick Rationale

  1. Gambas al ajillo is a Spanish tapa built on the principle that garlic + olive oil + chili + heat = a complete sauce — the technique is the recipe. Starting garlic in cold oil (confiting) extracts maximum flavor without burning, while the brief high-heat sear on the shrimp triggers Maillard browning for savory depth.
  2. Red Argentinian shrimp are naturally sweet and tender, so the entire cook is about not overcooking — pulling at 120°F and trusting carryover to 130°F keeps them succulent where most home cooks turn them to rubber at 160°F.
  3. Serving warm over cold salad greens creates a temperature contrast that makes both components more interesting, and the infused oil doubles as a warm vinaigrette.
