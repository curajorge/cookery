# Reina Pepiada

**Date:** 2026-04-01
**Serves:** 4 (2 people + leftovers for another day)
**Prep time:** 20 min | **Cook time:** 0 min (chicken is pre-cooked) | **Total:** 20 min
**Status:** Draft

## Ingredients

### Reina Pepiada Filling

| Ingredient | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Rotisserie chicken (shredded) | 450g | ~½ of a Whole Foods rotisserie bird, all white and dark meat pulled and shredded by hand |
| Ripe Hass avocado | 300g (2 medium) | Flesh only — should yield to gentle thumb pressure |
| Mayonnaise (full-fat) | 110g | Hellmann's/Best Foods — do not use light |
| Lime juice (fresh) | 30g (~2 limes) | Acid brightens the fat and prevents oxidation |

| Black pepper (freshly ground) | 2g | |

| Ingredient | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|

| Kosher salt (Diamond Crystal) | 5g | Mixed into water before adding flour |
| Neutral oil (for cooking) | 15g | For the skillet — avocado or canola |

## Method

### Arepas

1. **Make the dough.** Dissolve 5g salt in 360g warm water. Add 240g harina P.A.N. in a steady stream, stirring constantly. Let it rest 5 minutes — the pre-cooked corn flour needs time to fully hydrate and swell, giving you a smooth, workable dough. Knead briefly until no cracks form when you press a ball flat.

2. **Shape.** Divide dough into 8 portions (~75g each). Roll each into a ball, then flatten into discs about 1.5 cm thick and 9 cm diameter. If edges crack, the dough is too dry — wet your hands and knead in a few grams of water.

3. **Cook — two-stage for the best crust.** Heat a cast iron skillet or plancha over medium heat (175°C/350°F surface temp) with 15g oil. Cook arepas 5–6 minutes per side until a golden-brown crust forms — this is the Maillard reaction on the corn starches creating that signature toasty flavor. Then transfer to a 200°C/400°F oven for 8–10 minutes until they sound hollow when tapped. The oven stage cooks the interior through without burning the crust.

### Reina Pepiada

4. **Shred the chicken.** Pull 450g of meat from the rotisserie bird using two forks or your hands — a mix of white and dark meat gives the best texture and flavor. Shred into thin, irregular strands (not chunks). Dark meat's higher fat and collagen content keeps the filling moist.

5. **Mash the avocado.** In a large bowl, roughly mash the 300g avocado with a fork — you want some chunks remaining, not a purée. Immediately add the 30g lime juice and toss to coat. The citric acid slows enzymatic browning (polyphenol oxidase) and adds essential brightness.

6. **Build the filling.** Add the 110g mayonnaise, 60g minced onion, 8g garlic, 15g cilantro, and jalapeño if using. Fold everything together. Add the shredded chicken and mix until evenly coated. Season with 4g salt and 2g pepper. Taste — it should be rich, creamy, tangy, and well-salted. Adjust lime or salt as needed.

7. **Stuff the arepas.** Slice each arepa through the equator (like a pita) and fill generously with the reina pepiada. Serve immediately — the warm arepa against the cool, creamy filling is the whole point.

## Quick Rationale

Reina Pepiada is Venezuela's most iconic arepa filling — essentially a chicken-avocado salad bound with mayo, sharpened with lime. The ratio here is generous on avocado (about 2:3 avocado-to-chicken by weight), which is what makes it luxuriously creamy rather than dry and chicken-forward. The two-stage arepa cook (skillet then oven) gives you a crispy Maillard crust and a fully cooked, fluffy interior — skillet alone risks raw centers, oven alone skips the crust.

## Notes & Adjustments

- Store leftover filling in an airtight container with plastic wrap pressed directly against the surface (to block oxygen) — good for 1 day in the fridge
- Leftover arepa dough can be wrapped and refrigerated for 1 day — re-hydrate with a few grams of water if stiff
- Some Venezuelan cooks add a small amount of peas (petit pois) — not traditional in the original Reina Pepiada but common in some regions
- The remaining half of the rotisserie chicken can be used for another meal — strip it and store within 2 hours of purchase (USDA 2-hour rule)
